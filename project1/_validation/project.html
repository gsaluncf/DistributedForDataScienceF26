Project 1 - Building a Real-Time Ad Selection Pipeline

Distributed Systems for Data Science - New College of Florida

You are a data scientist at AdFlow, a mid-sized ad platform serving mobile publishers. Your job is to deploy the Lambda worker that sits in the middle of a real-time bidding pipeline - reading ad opportunities from a queue, running a quality-adjusted scoring function to pick winners, and posting results as fast as possible.

Part 1: Background - How Online Ads Actually Work

1.1 The Problem: Showing the Right Ad in Milliseconds

Every time you open an app or a website and see an advertisement, a small distributed computing event just happened. In the time it took the page to load - usually under 100 milliseconds - the platform answered a surprisingly hard question:

The Core Question

Given what we know about this user, this piece of content, and the advertisers currently competing for this slot - which ad should we show right now?

There are thousands of advertisers who might want this slot. Each has a different budget, a different target audience, and different restrictions on where their ads can appear. The platform has to evaluate all of them, rank them, and return a winner - before the user notices the page has loaded.

1.2 The Three Players

Three parties are involved in every ad that gets shown:

  • The Publisher - the app or website hosting the ad. They have a slot on the screen and want to fill it with something that earns them money without driving users away.
  • The Advertiser - a company competing to show their ad. An advertiser might say: "I want to reach fitness-interested users aged 25-40, and I will pay up to $5.00 per thousand impressions."
  • The Ad Platform - the system in the middle. It receives the opportunity from the publisher, collects bids from advertisers, picks a winner, and delivers the ad. This is what you are building.

1.3 What an Ad Opportunity Looks Like

When a user opens an app, the publisher sends the ad platform a structured signal. Advertisers have already pre-registered their bids for various audience segments, so the platform assembles a list of competing bids automatically:

{
  "opportunity_id": "a3f9c821",
  "timestamp": "2024-11-14T14:32:07Z",
  "user_id": "u_99182",
  "content_category": "sports",
  "device_type": "mobile",
  "region": "southeast",
  "bids": [
    { "advertiser_id": "adv_001", "bid_amount": 3.20, "category": "sportswear" },
    { "advertiser_id": "adv_002", "bid_amount": 4.10, "category": "fast_food" },
    { "advertiser_id": "adv_003", "bid_amount": 2.85, "category": "sportswear" }
  ]
}

Each bid represents an advertiser saying "I want this slot and I will pay this amount." The platform decides who wins.

1.4 Why the Highest Bid Doesn't Always Win

The obvious answer - take the highest bid - turns out to be bad business. In the example above, adv_002 is offering $4.10 but wants to show a fast food ad in the middle of a sports article. A user deep in sports content is unlikely to engage with that ad. Meanwhile, adv_001 is offering $3.20 for a sportswear ad - a far better fit.

Real ad platforms account for this by adjusting raw bids based on contextual signals. This adjustment is called a scoring function. Instead of ranking advertisers by raw bid amount, the platform computes a score for each bid that reflects both what the advertiser is willing to pay and how good a fit their ad is for this moment.

Key Concept: Scoring Over Raw Bids

score = bid_amount x relevance_multiplier x time_of_day_bonus x device_bonus

The winner is the advertiser with the highest score - not the highest raw bid.

1.5 Why This Requires Distributed Computing

A mid-sized ad platform might handle several million ad opportunities per hour - each one requiring bid evaluation in under 100 milliseconds. No single machine can keep up. The standard solution is to queue incoming opportunities and distribute them across many Lambda functions running in parallel.

Part 2: Your Task

You will build the complete pipeline end-to-end. Speed matters - ad selection results that arrive late are worthless. Your Lambda must consume each batch, compute a winner, and post a timestamped result before the next batch arrives.

2.1 The Flow

  1. You create your own SQS input queue, results queue, and DynamoDB table following the naming convention.
  2. You write and deploy a Lambda function that reads batches from the input queue, applies the scoring and selection logic, and posts results to the results queue with a processed_at timestamp.
  3. Your Lambda also persists each result to your DynamoDB table.
  4. You query DynamoDB to produce an analytical report.

2.2 The Opportunity Message Format

Field Type Description
opportunity_id string Unique identifier for this ad slot
timestamp ISO 8601 string When the opportunity occurred
user_id string Anonymized user identifier
content_category string Category of surrounding content: sports, news, entertainment, finance, lifestyle
device_type string mobile or desktop
region string northeast, southeast, midwest, west, international
bids list of objects Each bid has: advertiser_id, bid_amount (USD CPM), category

Each opportunity will have between 3 and 8 competing bids. Bid amounts range from $1.00 to $8.00 CPM.

2.3 The Result Message Format

Field Type Description
opportunity_id string Copied from the input message
winning_advertiser_id string The advertiser whose bid won
winning_bid_amount float The raw bid amount of the winner (USD CPM)
winning_score float The computed score that won the auction
score_margin float Winning score minus second-place score
processed_at ISO 8601 string Timestamp of when your Lambda processed this result

2.4 The Scoring Function

Scoring Formula

score = bid_amount x relevance_multiplier x time_bonus x device_bonus

Relevance Multiplier - rewards bids whose advertiser category matches the content being viewed:

Content Category Advertiser Category Multiplier
sports sportswear 1.4
sports energy_drink 1.3
finance fintech 1.5
finance insurance 1.3
entertainment streaming 1.4
entertainment gaming 1.3
lifestyle beauty 1.3
lifestyle travel 1.2
any any other combination 1.0

Time Bonus - applies based on the hour extracted from the opportunity's UTC timestamp:

Hour Range (UTC) Bonus Rationale
06:00 - 09:00 1.20 Morning commute - high mobile engagement
12:00 - 14:00 1.15 Lunch browsing
19:00 - 23:00 1.25 Evening peak - highest engagement
All other hours 1.00 No adjustment

Device Bonus - apply 1.1 for mobile, 1.0 for desktop.

Worked Example

Opportunity: content_category = sports, device_type = mobile, timestamp hour = 20:00 UTC.

Bid A: adv_001, bid_amount=3.50, category=sportswear
Bid B: adv_002, bid_amount=4.20, category=fast_food

Score A = 3.50 x 1.4 (sports/sportswear) x 1.25 (evening) x 1.1 (mobile) = 6.74
Score B = 4.20 x 1.0 (no match)          x 1.25 (evening) x 1.1 (mobile) = 5.78

Winner: adv_001 - despite submitting the lower raw bid

Part 4: The Bid Engine

The Lambda worker skeleton contains four functions to implement. The Lambda receives batches of opportunity messages from SQS, computes a winner for each, and posts results. Speed and correctness both matter.

Task 1: compute_score()

Implement the scoring formula from Section 2.4. The function receives a single bid dict and the full opportunity dict and returns a float score. Handle edge cases: what is the correct behavior if a category is not in the relevance lookup? What if a field is missing?

Task 2: select_winner()

Given all the bids for a single opportunity, return the winning bid along with its score and the second-highest score. Both scores are required fields in the result message.

Task 3: process_opportunity()

This is where throughput is determined. For every opportunity: parse the message, determine the winner, and post a result message to the results queue with all required fields from Section 2.3, including the processed_at timestamp. You must also persist the result to DynamoDB. Think carefully about the order of operations and what gets measured.

Task 4: lambda_handler()

A single malformed message must not stall the entire batch. Implement partial batch failure handling so that failed message IDs are returned to SQS for retry while the rest of the batch commits. Look up the SQS batchItemFailures response format - the key name and structure must be exact or SQS will retry the entire batch.

Part 5: Analyst Report

Once your Lambda has been running and results have accumulated in DynamoDB, query the table and produce a report. Your audience is the business analysts on the AdFlow team. They are not engineers - they want to understand how the selection algorithm is performing. Each finding should include the code that produced it and 2-3 sentences of plain-language interpretation.

Question 1: How Often Does the Highest Bidder Lose?

Across all 500 auctions, what percentage were won by an advertiser who did not submit the highest raw bid? Break this down by content category. Which categories show the largest gap between the highest bidder and the actual winner?

Question 2: Which Advertiser Categories Benefit Most From Scoring?

Compare each advertiser category's average raw bid to their win rate. Which categories win more often than their average bid would predict? Which win less often?

Question 3: Time of Day and Revenue

Plot total platform revenue (sum of winning bid amounts) by hour of day. Then plot average score margin by hour of day. During peak hours when the time bonus is active, does competition between advertisers appear to increase or decrease?

Question 4: The Fairness Question

A small advertiser with a typical bid of $2.00 argues that the scoring function still systematically favors deep-pocketed competitors - even when the small advertiser's content match is better. Using the data, evaluate this claim. Under what conditions, if any, can a $2.00 bid beat a $6.00 bid?

Hint

Work through the math first. An advertiser with bid_amount=2.00 and a perfect category match (multiplier=1.5) versus an advertiser with bid_amount=6.00 and no match (multiplier=1.0). What does each score look like? Then check whether this scenario actually occurs in your results.

A Note on What This Is

The company name and data are synthetic, but the system design is not. Google, Meta, and Amazon all operate pipelines structurally similar to what you are building. The scoring function here is a simplified version of what the industry calls quality-adjusted bidding or eCPM ranking. If this domain interests you, real-time bidding (RTB) and header bidding are worth reading about.

Building a Real-Time Ad Selection Pipeline

Deploy a Lambda-based bid engine that processes a live stream of ad auctions and routes results for analysis.

Part 1 Background — How Online Ads Actually Work

1.1 The Problem: Showing the Right Ad in Milliseconds

Every time you open an app or a website and see an advertisement, a small distributed computing event just happened. In the time it took the page to load — usually under 100 milliseconds — the platform answered a surprisingly hard question:

The Core Question

Given what we know about this user, this piece of content, and the advertisers currently competing for this slot — which ad should we show right now?

There are thousands of advertisers who might want this slot. Each has a different budget, a different target audience, and different restrictions on where their ads can appear. The platform has to evaluate all of them, rank them, and return a winner — before the user notices the page has loaded.

The full RTB flow from user action to ad delivery — all within ~100 ms

1.2 The Three Players

Three parties are involved in every ad that gets shown:

  • The Publisher — the app or website hosting the ad. They have a slot on the screen and want to fill it with something that earns them money without driving users away.
  • The Advertiser — a company competing to show their ad. An advertiser might say: "I want to reach fitness-interested users aged 25–40, and I will pay up to $5.00 per thousand impressions."
  • The Ad Platform — the system in the middle. It receives the opportunity from the publisher, collects bids from advertisers, picks a winner, and delivers the ad. This is what you are building.

1.3 What an Ad Opportunity Looks Like

When a user opens an app, the publisher sends the ad platform a structured signal containing everything known about that moment — who the user is, what content they are viewing, what device they are on, and where they are located. Advertisers have already pre-registered their bids for various audience segments, so the platform assembles a list of competing bids automatically:

{
  "opportunity_id":   "a3f9c821",
  "timestamp":        "2024-11-14T14:32:07Z",
  "user_id":          "u_99182",
  "content_category": "sports",
  "device_type":      "mobile",
  "region":           "southeast",
  "bids": [
    { "advertiser_id": "adv_001", "bid_amount": 3.20, "category": "sportswear" },
    { "advertiser_id": "adv_002", "bid_amount": 4.10, "category": "fast_food"  },
    { "advertiser_id": "adv_003", "bid_amount": 2.85, "category": "sportswear" }
  ]
}

Each bid represents an advertiser saying "I want this slot and I will pay this amount." The platform decides who wins.

1.4 Why the Highest Bid Doesn't Always Win

The obvious answer — take the highest bid — turns out to be bad business. Consider the example above: adv_002 is offering $4.10 but wants to show a fast food ad in the middle of a sports article. A user deep in sports content is unlikely to engage with that ad, which means the advertiser gets poor results and may reduce future bids. Meanwhile, adv_001 is offering $3.20 for a sportswear ad — a far better fit for the context.

Real ad platforms account for this by adjusting raw bids based on contextual signals. Time of day matters — users engage differently during a morning commute than late at night. Device type matters. Content relevance matters most of all. A slightly lower bid from a well-matched advertiser is worth more to everyone in the long run.

This adjustment is called a scoring function. Instead of ranking advertisers by raw bid amount, the platform computes a score for each bid that reflects both what the advertiser is willing to pay and how good a fit their ad is for this moment. The highest score wins.

Key Concept — Scoring Over Raw Bids

score = bid_amount × relevance_multiplier × time_of_day_bonus × device_bonus

The winner is the advertiser with the highest score — not the highest raw bid. This approach is called quality-adjusted bidding or eCPM ranking in the industry.

1.5 Why This Requires Distributed Computing

A mid-sized ad platform might handle several million ad opportunities per hour — each one requiring bid evaluation and score computation in under 100 milliseconds. No single machine can keep up.

The standard solution is to queue incoming opportunities and distribute them across many Lambda functions running in parallel. Each Lambda pulls a batch of messages from an input queue, runs the scoring and selection logic, and posts a timestamped result message to a results queue. A separate downstream consumer reads that results queue and persists each record to DynamoDB for later analysis. The queues act as connective tissue — decoupling the live stream from the processing, and the processing from storage.

In this assignment, the stream, both queues, the downstream consumer, and DynamoDB are all provided and already running. Your job is to deploy the Lambda in the middle and write the analyst report at the end.

2.1 The Flow

  1. An opportunity stream feeds the live SQS input queue. (Provided — do not modify.)
  2. Your Lambda reads batches from the input queue, applies the scoring and selection logic, and posts a result message — including a processed_at timestamp — to the SQS results queue.
  3. A downstream consumer reads the results queue and persists each result to DynamoDB. (Provided — do not modify.)
  4. You query DynamoDB to produce an analytical report for the business analysts who need to understand how the selection algorithm is performing.

2.2 The Opportunity Message Format

Each message your Lambda receives from the input queue is a JSON object with the following fields:

FieldTypeDescription
opportunity_idstringUnique identifier for this ad slot
timestampISO 8601 stringWhen the opportunity occurred
user_idstringAnonymized user identifier
content_categorystringCategory of surrounding content: sports, news, entertainment, finance, lifestyle
device_typestringmobile or desktop
regionstringnortheast, southeast, midwest, west, international
bidslist of objectsEach bid has: advertiser_id, bid_amount (USD CPM), category

Each opportunity will have between 3 and 8 competing bids. Bid amounts range from $1.00 to $8.00 CPM.

2.3 The Result Message Format

For each opportunity processed, your Lambda must post one result message to RESULTS_QUEUE_URL. The downstream consumer reads from that queue and persists records to DynamoDB — your Lambda never writes to DynamoDB directly. The result must include:

FieldTypeDescription
opportunity_idstringCopied from the input message
winning_advertiser_idstringThe advertiser whose bid won
winning_bid_amountfloatThe raw bid amount of the winner (USD CPM)
winning_scorefloatThe computed score that won the auction
score_marginfloatWinning score minus second-place score
processed_atISO 8601 stringTimestamp of when your Lambda posted this result — used to measure latency
Where the result goes

Your Lambda calls sqs.send_message(QueueUrl=RESULTS_QUEUE_URL, MessageBody=json.dumps(result)). That is the only output. The downstream consumer handles everything after that.

2.4 The Scoring Function

Your Lambda must evaluate every bid in a batch using the following scoring function. Batches arrive with multiple opportunities, each carrying multiple bids — structure your code to handle them efficiently across both dimensions.

Scoring Formula

score = bid_amount × relevance_multiplier × time_bonus × device_bonus

Relevance Multiplier

Rewards bids whose advertiser category matches the content being viewed. Any combination not listed receives 1.0 (no adjustment).

Content CategoryAdvertiser CategoryMultiplier
sportssportswear1.4
sportsenergy_drink1.3
financefintech1.5
financeinsurance1.3
entertainmentstreaming1.4
entertainmentgaming1.3
lifestylebeauty1.3
lifestyletravel1.2
anyany other combination1.0

Time Bonus

Applied based on the UTC hour of the opportunity timestamp. These windows correspond to peak engagement periods in mobile usage data.

Hour Range (UTC)BonusRationale
06:00 – 09:001.20Morning commute — high mobile engagement
12:00 – 14:001.15Lunch browsing
19:00 – 23:001.25Evening peak — highest engagement across platforms
All other hours1.00No adjustment

Device Bonus

Apply 1.1 for mobile, 1.0 for desktop.

Worked Example

Opportunity: content_category=sports, device_type=mobile, timestamp hour = 20:00 UTC. Two competing bids:

Bid A: advertiser_id=adv_001, bid_amount=3.50, category=sportswear
Bid B: advertiser_id=adv_002, bid_amount=4.20, category=fast_food

Score A = 3.50 × 1.4 (sports/sportswear) × 1.25 (evening) × 1.1 (mobile) = 6.73
Score B = 4.20 × 1.0 (no match)          × 1.25 (evening) × 1.1 (mobile) = 5.78

Winner: adv_001 — despite submitting the lower raw bid

A well-designed scoring function systematically shifts winners away from pure spending power toward contextual fit. Your analysis in Part 5 will measure exactly how much this happens across the full dataset.

Getting your pipeline running is itself part of the assignment. You will configure real AWS infrastructure, deploy your Lambda, and verify that all the pieces are communicating before any analysis is possible. Work through the steps below in order — each one is a prerequisite for the next.

3.2 Deployment Steps

1

Create Your DynamoDB Table

The downstream consumer writes to a DynamoDB table that you create. Use opportunity_id (String) as the partition key. The table name you choose becomes the DYNAMO_TABLE_NAME environment variable on your Lambda. Make sure the attribute types match the result message schema from Section 2.3 exactly — the consumer expects them and will fail silently if they do not match.

2

Deploy Your Lambda

Package your code and upload to AWS Lambda:

pip install -r requirements.txt -t ./package
cp worker/lambda_handler.py ./package/
cd package && zip -r ../deployment.zip . && cd ..

Configure the function with: runtime Python 3.11, memory 256 MB, timeout 30 seconds, execution role set to LAMBDA_EXECUTION_ROLE_ARN, and environment variables: INPUT_QUEUE_URL, RESULTS_QUEUE_URL, DYNAMO_TABLE_NAME, AWS_DEFAULT_REGION.

3

Wire the SQS Trigger

Add INPUT_QUEUE_URL as an event source for your Lambda. Key settings:

  • Batch size: 10 — your Lambda receives up to 10 opportunity messages per invocation
  • Batch window: 5 seconds — SQS waits up to 5 seconds to fill a batch before invoking
  • Report batch item failures: enabled — required for Task 4 partial failure handling to work
4

Verify End-to-End

Before writing any bid logic, confirm the pipeline is wired correctly:

  • CloudWatch Logs — your Lambda should be invoking without errors
  • Results queue — messages should appear after each invocation
  • DynamoDB — rows should accumulate as the consumer processes the results queue

If any of these checks fail, the problem is in the wiring, not your code. Fix the infrastructure first.

Part 4 The Bid Engine

With your infrastructure running, the bid selection logic is what determines correctness. The Lambda worker skeleton contains four functions to implement. Speed and correctness both matter — results posted with stale timestamps are flagged in the grading evaluation.

Task 1 implement_scoring_function(bid, opportunity)

Implement the scoring formula from Section 2.4. The function receives a single bid dict and the full opportunity dict and returns a float score. Handle edge cases carefully: what is the correct behavior if a category is not in the relevance lookup? What if a required field is missing from the message?

Task 2 select_winner(bids, opportunity)

Given all the bids for a single opportunity, return the winning bid along with its score and the second-highest score. Both scores are required fields in the result message posted to the results queue.

Task 3 process_batch(event, context)

This is where throughput is determined. Your Lambda receives a batch of SQS messages — each one an opportunity carrying multiple bids. For every message: parse the opportunity, determine the winner, and post a result message to RESULTS_QUEUE_URL with all required fields from Section 2.3, including the processed_at timestamp. The downstream consumer reads from that queue and persists to DynamoDB — your Lambda never writes to DynamoDB directly.

Think carefully about how you structure the work across a batch with many opportunities and many bids per opportunity — the way you organize this computation will affect both speed and readability.

Task 4 handle_failures()

A single malformed message must not stall the entire batch. Implement partial batch failure handling so that failed message IDs are returned to SQS for retry while the rest of the batch commits successfully. Look up the SQS batchItemFailures response format — the key name and structure must be exact or SQS will retry the entire batch regardless.

Part 5 Analyst Report

Once your Lambda has been running and results have accumulated in DynamoDB, query the table and produce a report for the business analysts on the AdFlow team. They are not engineers — they want to understand how the selection algorithm is performing, whether it is behaving as intended, and whether there are any fairness concerns worth raising.

Your report lives in analysis/analysis.ipynb. Each finding should include the code that produced it and 2–3 sentences of plain-language interpretation written for someone who understands business metrics but will not be reading your Python.

Question 1 — How Often Does the Highest Bidder Lose?

Across all processed opportunities, what percentage of auctions were won by an advertiser who did not submit the highest raw bid? Break this down by content category. Which categories show the largest gap between the highest bidder and the actual winner?

Question 2 — Which Advertiser Categories Benefit Most From Scoring?

Compare each advertiser category's average raw bid to their win rate. Which categories win more often than their average bid would predict? Which win less often? What does this tell you about the scoring function's design choices?

Question 3 — Time of Day and Revenue

Plot total platform revenue (sum of winning bid amounts) by hour of day. Then plot average score margin by hour of day. During peak hours when the time bonus is active, does competition between advertisers appear to increase or decrease? What are the implications for platform pricing strategy?

Question 4 — The Fairness Question

A small advertiser with a typical bid of $2.00 argues that the scoring function still systematically favors deep-pocketed competitors — even when their content match is better. Using the data, evaluate this claim. Under what conditions, if any, can a $2.00 bid beat a $6.00 bid?

Hint

Work through the math first. An advertiser with bid_amount=2.00 and a perfect category match (multiplier=1.5) versus one with bid_amount=6.00 and no match (multiplier=1.0). What does each score look like across different time windows and device types? Then check whether this scenario actually occurs in your results and how often the lower bidder wins when the relevance gap is at its maximum.

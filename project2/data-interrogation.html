Getting the Data
How many rows are in your dataset? How many columns?
Not a vague sense. The actual number. If you can rattle this off from memory, you're already ahead of most working data scientists.
Click to reveal
A model trained on 200 rows behaves fundamentally differently from one trained on 200,000 rows. If you're building a classifier with 12 classes but only have 50 examples per class, your model will overfit on noise. Knowing the shape is knowing what's possible.
Getting the Data
What does one row represent?
One row represents ___. A single transaction? A daily average? A user session? A sensor reading every 5 seconds? If you can't fill in that blank precisely, your silver-to-gold transformation is at risk.
Click to reveal
Aggregation errors are the #1 silent killer of data pipelines. If your bronze table has one row per sensor reading but your model expects one row per hour, your transformation must aggregate correctly. Getting the grain wrong means your model learns from a fiction.
Getting the Data
Is there fresher data available for your problem?
Stale data steals your sizzle. An API that hasn't been called since March, a Kaggle CSV from 2019, a scrape you ran once and never refreshed. If newer data exists and is accessible, updating your pipeline now is one of the highest-impact moves you can make before submission.
Click to reveal
Concept drift is real. A model trained on pre-pandemic restaurant data will make terrible predictions about 2026 dining patterns. For your write-up, being able to state "this data covers January 2024 through March 2026" shows awareness. State the temporal bounds of your data in your write-up. It's a sign of professional maturity.
Shaping the Data
What percentage of your most important column has missing values?
Pick the single column your model depends on most. Your target variable or your strongest feature. Do you know its null rate?
Click to reveal
Many modeling frameworks silently drop rows with nulls, or fill them with a default. If 30% of your key column is null, you may be training on a biased 70% subset without knowing it. You must make a conscious decision.
Shaping the Data
Do you have any duplicate rows? How would you know?
What's your primary key or unique identifier? Have you run a GROUP BY and HAVING COUNT(*) > 1 check on it?
Click to reveal
Duplicate records inflate the importance of certain observations. In a training set, duplicates bias the model toward those examples. Your silver layer should be the deduplication boundary.
Shaping the Data
Are your columns the right data types?
Is the "price" column actually a float, or is it a string with dollar signs? Type mismatches are one of the most common sources of silent errors in pipelines.
Click to reveal
A column that looks numeric but is stored as a string will pass through your pipeline without complaint. Spark is especially unforgiving: if your bronze schema infers "price" as StringType, every downstream aggregation on that column is wrong. Run df.dtypes or df.printSchema() and verify every column.
Shaping the Data
Have you looked at the distribution of your key features?
Not just the mean. The actual shape. Is it bell-curved, skewed, bimodal? A price column where 99% of values are under $100 but one entry is $1,000,000 will distort everything.
Click to reveal
Most ML algorithms assume your features are reasonably distributed. Run df.describe() on your gold table and look at the min, max, and standard deviation. If the max is 100x the mean, you probably have outliers to handle.
Understanding the Data
Who or what might be underrepresented in your data?
If your dataset is restaurant reviews, small towns are probably invisible. If it's weather data, ocean-adjacent sensors dominate. What slice of reality is your data quietly ignoring?
Click to reveal
The most dangerous model is the confident one that's never seen your test case.
Understanding the Data
What time period does your data cover? What's outside that window?
2020 data includes a pandemic. Summer data doesn't know about winter. Understanding your data's time horizon is a sign of real analytical maturity.
Click to reveal
Ask: would my model's predictions change if I shifted the data window by 6 months? If yes, you have temporal bias.
Understanding the Data
Is your target variable balanced?
If you're doing classification, what's the split? 90/10 is common, and it means your model can get 90% accuracy by always predicting the majority class.
Click to reveal
Choose a metric that's honest about your class distribution, not one that flatters it.
Understanding the Data
Could any of your features be secretly leaking the answer?
Feature leakage happens when a column encodes information that wouldn't exist at prediction time. Think carefully about what your model would know before the event you're predicting.
Click to reveal
If your model accuracy seems too good to be true, audit your features for leakage.
Modeling & Shipping
If a user sends your model a totally unexpected input, what happens?
Negative temperatures for an ice cream predictor. A restaurant with 0 reviews. Thinking through edge cases now makes your app way more robust.
Click to reveal
Your UI should make it hard to send bad input, and your API should handle it when someone does anyway.
Modeling & Shipping
If you re-ran your entire pipeline from scratch right now, would you get the exact same gold table?
Same row count? Same values? Same column types? If not, it's worth figuring out what would shift.
Click to reveal
If you can't reproduce your data, you can't trust your model.
Modeling & Shipping
What's the one step in your pipeline you'd most want to strengthen?
An API rate limit. A hardcoded file path. A column name that might change. Every pipeline has a spot that could use a bit more love.

Know your single point of failure.
